# Slide archetypes

Each archetype is a slide pattern proven in TED-style decks. Pick exactly one per beat. If two archetypes seem to fit, the beat probably contains two ideas — split.

## 1. full-bleed-image

Hero photograph filling the entire slide, edge to edge. Optional 0–3 word caption.

**Use for:**
- Atmospheric establishing beats
- Metaphor (image stands in for an abstract concept)
- Emotional anchor that cannot be carried by words alone

**Image prompt pattern:**
> Photorealistic cinematic [subject], [composition: close-up / medium / wide], [lighting: golden hour / overcast / dramatic side-light], [mood: contemplative / urgent / triumphant], shot on [lens cue: 85mm / 35mm / wide-angle], shallow depth of field, no text overlay, [aspect 16:9].

**Caption rule:** If a caption is needed, max 3 words. If you cannot say it in 3 words, use a `typographic` slide instead.

**Anti-patterns:**
- Cropped image with white border — defeats the bleed
- Stock-photo handshake / fake-smile humans
- Generic "team meeting" or "abstract tech" imagery

---

## 2. big-number

A single statistic dominates the slide. Optional one-line label below.

**Use for:**
- Single most important data point in the talk
- Scale shock (e.g. "1.4 billion")
- Quantitative anchor for an emotional argument

**Composition:**
- Number occupies 60–70% of slide width, vertically centered
- Label (if present): one line, sentence-length max, below the number
- Optional muted background image

**Image prompt pattern (if background used):**
> Subtle out-of-focus [subject related to number's meaning], desaturated, dark vignette so foreground number reads cleanly, [aspect 16:9].

**Anti-patterns:**
- Multiple numbers on one slide (split)
- Number with units in fine print (units belong in the label or be incorporated into the number itself)
- Pie chart "supporting" the number — pick one or the other

---

## 3. quote

A pull-quote with attribution.

**Use for:**
- Authority handoff — let an expert/customer/stakeholder make the point
- Emotional credibility
- Bridging from one section to another via a third voice

**Composition:**
- Quote: max 2 lines, 12–18 words
- Attribution: one line, smaller, below the quote
- Optional small portrait of the source

**Anti-patterns:**
- Quote longer than 2 lines (paraphrase or split)
- "—" as attribution prefix when name is missing
- Decorative quotation marks larger than the quote itself

---

## 4. typographic

1–5 word statement. Typography-driven. No image.

**Use for:**
- Big idea declaration
- Section thesis
- Punchline beat

**Composition:**
- Text occupies 40–60% of slide width
- High-contrast color (foreground vs background)
- Single weight, single typeface, sentence case unless brand demands otherwise

**Anti-patterns:**
- More than 5 words (use multiple slides)
- Decorative underlines, gradients, drop shadows
- Mixing weights/sizes for emphasis (let the words carry it)

---

## 5. chart

Streamlined data visualization with one highlighted takeaway.

**Use for:**
- Trend that is core to the argument
- Comparison where the magnitude itself is the point

**Composition:**
- One chart per slide (never two)
- Title: 4–8 words, names the takeaway, not the data ("Adoption tripled in Q3", not "Q3 Metrics")
- One element highlighted via color, scale, or annotation
- All other data desaturated or removed

**Anti-patterns:**
- Default chart legend with 6+ series
- Chart and a table of the same data
- 3D effects, gratuitous gridlines, overflowing axis labels

---

## 6. contrast-pair

Two paired images or concepts side-by-side. Duarte's what-is vs what-could-be made literal.

**Use for:**
- Before/after
- Status quo vs proposed alternative
- Problem vs solution at a glance

**Composition:**
- Two equal halves, separated by a thin gap or rule
- Each half has one image + one short label (1–3 words)
- Label color/weight may differ between halves to encode "bad" vs "good"

**Image prompt pattern:**
> Two prompts emitted as a pair. Match composition framing (both close-up, or both wide). Vary only the subject state.

**Anti-patterns:**
- Asymmetric framing (one close-up, one wide) reads as accidental
- Three-way contrast (use 2 or use steps)
- Long labels that turn the slide into a comparison table

---

## 7. section-divider

Chapter break in the narrative.

**Use for:**
- Major story transition (act break)
- Reset audience attention before a new section

**Composition:**
- Section title: 2–6 words
- Optional muted background image or solid brand color
- Optional small section number ("II", "Three") for orientation

**Anti-patterns:**
- Using as filler when no real section break exists
- Long titles that read as their own slide content

---

## 8. blank

Intentional empty slide. No image. No text. Solid color or black.

**Use for:**
- Speaker focus moment — eyes on the speaker, not the screen
- Pacing breath after a heavy data sequence
- Pre-STAR moment silence

**Composition:**
- Solid black or solid brand neutral
- Nothing else

**Anti-patterns:**
- Logo "watermark" in corner (defeats the purpose)
- Using more than 1–2 times per deck (loses impact)

---

## Selection guide

For each beat, ask in order:

1. Is this beat carrying emotional weight that words cannot? → `full-bleed-image`
2. Is the entire point a single number? → `big-number`
3. Is the speaker handing off authority to a third voice? → `quote`
4. Is the point a 1–5 word declaration? → `typographic`
5. Is there a data trend that IS the argument? → `chart`
6. Is the beat literally "X vs Y"? → `contrast-pair`
7. Is the deck transitioning to a new act? → `section-divider`
8. Should the audience look at the speaker, not the screen? → `blank`

If two answers say yes, the beat has two ideas. Split into two slides.

## STAR moment archetype mapping

The STAR moment is the most memorable beat in the talk. Pick its archetype deliberately:

- **Visual STAR** (Bill Gates mosquitos): `full-bleed-image` with unexpected subject
- **Quantitative STAR** (scale shock): `big-number` with a number 10x bigger than the audience expects
- **Linguistic STAR** (the line that gets quoted): `typographic` with the exact phrase
- **Demonstration STAR** (Jill Bolte Taylor brain): may be `blank` to put attention on the speaker performing the demonstration

The skill should flag when no archetype assignment registers as a STAR candidate.
